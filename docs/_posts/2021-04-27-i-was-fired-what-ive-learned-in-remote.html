It’s not an easy thing to say that someone didn’t want you. I’ve gone back and forth between saying it was mutual, it was for the best, and hey, they just plain fired me.

I’ve had many a time to reflect on what I’ve learned since being fired from a top management consulting and information technology services firm. I’ve tried to distill the lessons from the shift into a remote workforce and what were some of the causes of failure for me. Here goes:

1. Accountability 

Not having your boss “looming” over your shoulder can be good reason to tend to slack off. It’s easy to set your status to available and be completely engaged in another task. For me, I’d just feel at an im passe and then not be as active to resolve it as talking with someone in person. I’m a social nut. Face to face is my sweet spot. Texting doesn’t give you the same connection that in person conversation does. ;).

2. Resourcefulness 

It’s a plainly stated fact that you don’t have the same bandwidth of resources available to you when working from home. That’s where self-startership comes into play. I’d be hustling into the deep bowels of the internet to find a solution when a quick “hey, you ever faced this?” would’ve brought about the same—if not a better—solution. Having those team dynamics working in your favor takes increased proactivity and sometimes more conversation to get to the same place that you would’ve in a shorter time in person.

3. When to take the foot off the gas 

And then there’s the concern for breaks. The social cues for, “hey, now might be a good time to use the restroom or grab a coffee or tea” are just not present at home. Some productivity and worker-bee leaning people can get zoned into work and not come up for air. This was frequently me. Imagine trying to make a free throw and then deciding to take a water break before you’ve finished it. Part of you thinks that you’ve lost momentum when you return. However, it’s so critical to know that the solution that may seem unlikely—or even unknown—to you while in the heat of accomplishing something may come to you when you’re in the come up for air stage. 

The changes from the new work from home landscape have made it harder to read the social cues that allow you to stay productive and avoid distractions. It takes increased mindfulness and sometimes more back and forth to reach the same goal that an in person conversation would reach. The opportunity for improving the work from home workforce relies on improving the ability to maximize on the social cues that an in person environment creates.
